Thoughts on "24 Types of Business Models"

CLICK HERE TO VIEW "How to Build Revenue: 24 Types of Business Models with Examples"


Building a successful software product is more than just creating something cool; it also requires a sustainable business model. As an engineer, I naturally gravitate toward developing innovative solutions, but thinking about how to monetize my product has been a challenge. However, after reviewing different strategies, I have gained new insights into how I might structure my business in a way that aligns with both my goals and the needs of my users.


One potential approach is what I’ve come to think of as the "landlord" model. My software is designed to mimic community spaces, making it possible to implement a system where users can "rent" online spaces. This could allow me to offer different tiers of access, with larger and smaller virtual rooms based on a flexible pricing structure. Instead of forcing users into rigid payment plans, this approach could provide them with the ability to scale their usage according to their needs. This model intrigues me because it balances revenue generation with the community-oriented vision of my product.


Another common approach is ad-based monetization, which is a tempting option due to its accessibility. However, ads have a reputation for being intrusive and frustrating, and many users actively avoid platforms that rely too heavily on them. Furthermore, my software caters to a niche audience, limiting the advertising potential. Without a large enough user base to attract high-quality advertisers, this model may not be the most effective way to sustain the business.


I’ve also considered the subscription model, but I hesitate to rely on it entirely. Subscription fatigue is real, and many people are wary of signing up for yet another recurring payment. Trust in subscription-based services has been eroded over time, making it harder to convince potential users to commit. While a subscription model can provide stable revenue, I would need to offer significant value to justify the ongoing cost.


A hybrid approach might be the best path forward. By combining elements of different models, I can create a system that offers both free and premium experiences. The freemium model is particularly appealing because it allows users to access the software at no cost while providing incentives to upgrade for additional features. Given that my target demographic doesn’t have a lot of disposable income to spend on this hobby, it’s important to offer value without making the product feel inaccessible. I envision providing smaller, free spaces while reserving larger, customizable spaces for paying users. This structure would ensure that everyone can participate while still generating revenue from those who want more functionality.


Although I haven’t finalized my business model, I’m feeling optimistic. The key takeaway from my research is that there isn’t a one-size-fits-all solution, and flexibility will be crucial. By testing different approaches and listening to user feedback, I can refine my strategy over time. I have no reason to believe that I can’t find a sustainable way to support my venture while staying true to my vision. The challenge of monetization is just another part of the creative process, and I’m excited to explore the possibilities.


Thanks for reading!
